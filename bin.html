Nerfies: Deformable Neural Radiance Fields

Keunhong Park1, Utkarsh Sinha2, Jonathan T. Barron2, Sofien Bouaziz2, Dan B Goldman2, Steven M. Seitz1,2, Ricardo Martin-Brualla2
1University of Washington, 2Google Research
Paper arXiv Video Code Data

Nerfies turns selfie videos from your phone into free-viewpoint portraits.

Abstract

We present the first method capable of photorealistically reconstructing a non-rigidly deforming scene using photos/videos captured casually from mobile phones.

Our approach augments neural radiance fields (NeRF) by optimizing an additional continuous volumetric deformation field that warps each observed point into a canonical 5D NeRF. We observe that these NeRF-like deformation fields are prone to local minima, and propose a coarse-to-fine optimization method for coordinate-based models that allows for more robust optimization. By adapting principles from geometry processing and physical simulation to NeRF-like models, we propose an elastic regularization of the deformation field that further improves robustness.

We show that Nerfies can turn casually captured selfie photos/videos into deformable NeRF models that allow for photorealistic renderings of the subject from arbitrary viewpoints, which we dub "nerfies". We evaluate our method by collecting data using a rig with two mobile phones that take time-synchronized photos, yielding train/validation images of the same pose at different viewpoints. We show that our method faithfully reconstructs non-rigidly deforming scenes and reproduces unseen views with high fidelity.

Topological evaluation of Causal and Temporal Reasoning in Large Vision-Language Models

Giuliano Campagnolo
Supervisors: Elisa Ricci, Paolo Rota, Benedetta Libertadori
Bachelor Thesis (2026) | University of Trento
Paper Code

A zero-shot evaluation framework based on Task Graphs for analyzing procedural videos in Large Vision-Language Models.

Abstract

Evaluating the capacity for temporal and causal reasoning in modern Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs) frequently encounters the limitations of current benchmarks. In the temporal reordering test, known as "Shuffle and Reorder", the standard evaluation assumes the existence of a single correct sequence, imposing a strictly linear Ground Truth. This rigidity is fundamentally unsuitable for real-world procedural activities (such as cooking recipes), where various sub-sequences of actions are logically commutative or entirely independent. Consequently, evaluating a model by requiring perfect correspondence with the original video sequence ends up penalizing the model's intelligence, labeling logically valid procedural variants as failures.

To overcome these critical issues, we introduce a novel zero-shot evaluation framework based on Task Graphs, formalized as Directed Acyclic Graphs (DAGs). The main contribution consists of abandoning metrics based on positional distance in favor of topological measurements, specifically Closure Accuracy and Reduction Accuracy. This approach serves to separate the execution order (an arbitrary choice made by the actor) from the dependency order (an intrinsic causal constraint of the problem), exclusively evaluating violations of causal logic. The framework was applied to procedural videos extracted from the CaptainCook4D and COMKITCHENS datasets.

Furthermore, to rigorously quantify the actual performance gain compared to pure chance, a Random Baseline was empirically calculated through Monte Carlo simulation. Performance was analyzed by testing two state-of-the-art models (InternVL3.5-8B and Qwen2.5-VL-7B-Instruct) while crossing two independent variables: visual granularity (number of frames per clip) and cognitive complexity (number of clips). The experimental analysis led to three main conclusions. First, a clear architectural gap emerged: InternVL3.5-8B demonstrated solid deductive capabilities, maintaining an advantage over pure chance even on complex sequences, while Qwen2.5-VL-7B-Instruct highlighted structural limits, performing statistically worse than random reordering. Second, increasing the sampled frames per single clip did not produce tangible improvements, demonstrating that the bottleneck lies in the logical processing capacity of the global context rather than in instantaneous visual perception. Finally, qualitative analysis revealed a strong dependence on visual biases: models excel in the presence of clear and irreversible state changes, but struggle to deduce the causal flow in scenarios with minimal or ambiguous visual variations.

Video Presentation

The Shuffle and Reorder Problem

One of the generative tests used by the scientific community to measure true causal comprehension is temporal reordering, also known as Video Jigsaw or Shuffle and Reorder. By providing a sequence of randomly shuffled clips, the model is forced to understand the logical dependencies between the frames to reconstruct their original chronology.

However, traditional approaches share a structural limitation: they assume a strictly linear Ground Truth. We introduce a fundamental distinction, separating the execution order (the arbitrary choice made by the actor in the video) from the true dependency order (the intrinsic causal constraint of the test).

Task Graphs (DAGs)

To model the intrinsic flexibility of human activities, we adopt Task Graphs, formalized as Directed Acyclic Graphs (DAGs). In this paradigm, nodes represent the steps of the activity and edges exclusively encode the necessary precedence relations.

We utilize Task Graphs as a benchmark to measure the zero-shot capabilities of pre-trained LVLMs, transitioning from linear ranking metrics to topological coherence.

Topological Metrics

Closure Accuracy

Closure Accuracy is the primary metric of this study. It measures the percentage of total dependency relations (direct and indirect) correctly respected by the predicted sequence. This metric is robust because it penalizes only violations of causal logic, ignoring the relative order of independent actions.

Reduction Accuracy

For a complete analysis, we also calculate the Reduction Accuracy, which focuses exclusively on immediate dependencies represented by the transitive reduction of the reference graph. By comparing Closure and Reduction Accuracy, we assess whether a model fails predominantly on distant logical connections or immediate procedural steps.


Experimental Setup & Results

We evaluated the models on the CaptainCook4D and COMKITCHENS datasets, comparing performance against a rigorous Monte Carlo Random Baseline.

Architectural Gap: InternVL3.5-8B demonstrated solid deductive capabilities, systematically outperforming the baseline. Conversely, Qwen2.5-VL-7B-Instruct suffered a performance collapse below random chance as sequence length increased.

Visual Density Paradox: Increasing the sampled frames per clip (from 2 to 8) did not yield tangible improvements, indicating that the bottleneck resides in logical context processing rather than instantaneous visual perception.

Visual Bias Dependence: LVLMs excel when presented with distinct, irreversible state changes but struggle severely in scenarios with minimal or ambiguous visual variations.
